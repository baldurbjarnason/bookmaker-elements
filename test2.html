Better EPUB export from Adobe InDesign

The single most important fact to keep in mind about the computer is that it's blind.

It can't "see" the headings or blockquotes even though that's obvious to humans.

To create ebooks and websites that a computer and its app understand we need to make sure that it's told exactly what everything is.

We need to make sure that every important paragraph and character style is mapped to a tag that tells the computer what that bit of text actually is.

Adobe's guide for how to do that in InDesign is here: http://www.adobe.com/accessibility/products/indesign/mapping.html

If we don't, a couple of services that the reader depends on will fall apart:

  • Styles will render incorrectly in many ebook readers (B&N, Kobo, Marvin).
  • Accessibility for the blind will be non-existent both in the EPUB and in the PDF. We have customers who get PDF versions of the books with their pledges and unless the tagging is done in InDesign those PDFs will not be accessible.
  • Internal navigation to important parts of the ebook (ToC, Forewords/Introductions, Indexes) is compromised or disabled in reading apps that support them (many Kindles, for example).

—So what do you need to do?

Simple. Use the following checklists and you should be good to go.

Tag mapping checklist

  1. Use InDesign features to create lists and tables instead of mimicking their look manually. DO NOT include tables as images.
  2. h1. The title of the book on the earliest title page should be mapped to the h1 tag. It is a signal for browsers as to what the title of the book is.
  3. h2 is what you use for chapter headings, both the chapter title and the chapter number (if applicable). If you extracted all of the h2 tags in the book the list should form a meaningful and accurate outline of its structure.
  4. Map sub-headings to h3, including chapter sub-titles. If you extracted all of the h3 tags in a chapter the list should form a meaningful and accurate outline of that chapter's structure.
  5. All italic character styles should be mapped to em (which is short for "emphasis").
  6. All bold character styles should be mapped to strong (which stands for "strong emphasis").
  7. All separators (e.g. centred asterisks) must have "Separator" set as a class in Export Tagging.
  8. All quotations and poetry must have a class set in Export Tagging that begins with "Quote-" ("Quote" and a hyphen). This lets us convert runs of quoted text into proper HTML blockquotes on our end.

EPUB export checklist

Adobe's export guide: https://helpx.adobe.com/indesign/using/export-content-epub-cc.html

  1. Choose EPUB (Reflowable).
  2. Choose "3.0" as the version.
  3. 'Remove Forced Line Breaks' is checked. Options > Remove Forced Line Breaks
  4. Make sure that the resolution of the images is high enough for mobile phone use, at least 200 ppi, no more than 300 ppi. Conversion Settings > Resolution (PPI)
  5. All of the footnotes are proper EPUB 3.0 footnotes: select "Inside a Pop-up (EPUB3)" in Text > Footnotes.
  6. JPEG Options > Image Quality is at least Medium.
  7. Object > Preserve Appearance from Layout is checked, because we want the images to look the same in the ebook as it does in PDF.
  8. CSS > Include Embeddable Fonts must NOT be checked.

All images MUST have descriptive alternate text

This is essential for the accessibility of both the ebook and the PDF.

Adobe's guide for how to do that is here: https://www.adobe.com/accessibility/products/indesign/alt-text.html

  1. Purely decorative images (e.g. flourishes and custom separators) do not need alternative text.
  2. All photographs need alternative text.
  3. All drawings and illustrations need alternative text.

Keep the text short and descriptive. Do not go into excessive detail.
